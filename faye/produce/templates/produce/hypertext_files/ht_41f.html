I am at a loss. And even though I am in a forest I feel surrounded by nothing alive. The kabocha feels incredibly far away.
But I remember the lemon soda, so long ago now. I check the coat pocket and it is still there, almost finished. I ask you if you will accept the soda.
You begin to talk of summer, its heat and brightness, up there in your cold dark tree. Of your childhood taking lemons from your family’s trees and biting into them. You talk of buying me the soda in the city because it was easier, because you could grow no lemons to bite, and the supermarket’s were pale and waxy. How you worried that I would find out one day that you did this, and that I would love you less for not being able to provide.
You are lost in your memories. I think to comfort you.
But instead I interrupt you to ask politely to leave.
I offer the seed to you, and you accept it. You begin to talk about our garden, but I know it is both you and not you. I also know that, when you are lost in memory, it means I have given you what was required. You pine for days passed.
And like all ghosts, you keep your promises.
