“Is there a stone you like best?” the kabocha asks, “Pick one.”
We walk up the hill. I push my dad’s coat back over my shoulders, holding the kabocha with two hands now in the chilly open air. There is no wind, and so I don’t worry about its eyes. In fact, I am not sure what I should be worrying about. The ground is firm but nowhere level.
I choose a medium-sized, four-sided grave marker. Its precise edges have begun to round against the seasons.
“Is here all right?” I ask the kabocha.
“Here is perfect,” I hear from my hands. “Place me at its base now.”
I neatly place the kabocha at the base of the grave stone, turning it so that its eyes face outward. I look into them again, and I can find no difference in the candles, whose languid flames flow around like water in its eyes.
“It is like the ocean out here, isn’t it,” says the kabocha, seemingly seeing my thoughts.
“These fields of grass,” it says, “bending under the wind, always remind me of the rolling surface of the sea. Or how I imagine it must be. And I always imagined that, when set down here, it would be like floating in a vast ocean.”
“And is it?” I ask.
The kabocha pumpkin’s eyes bend to the left and to the right, under forces I cannot see.
“I have never seen the sea. But like I said, child, this is strange grass.”
