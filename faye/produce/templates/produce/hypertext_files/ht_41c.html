“Hello, child,” you say to me. Your arms are twisted up in the tree. Your body is supported by its thick limbs. You are part of it, but you are also who I remember.
“Come closer,” you say, “You have come all this way.”
I keep my distance. I ask you what you want. What it will take for me to leave here.
“You would leave your mother?”
You are not my mother. Or, at least, it is more complicated than that. When the world becomes thin, it seems like people–who they are, what they do—can slip from place to place, reflecting off and multiplying in the weak barriers that transect our lives. I can’t tell from where your body reflects now.
“What must I do to leave?”
“You must give me three things.”
I consider what I have. I tell you that I can give you one of the cigarettes in my pocket.
“They are your father’s?” you ask.
I tell you yes, and you accept, and tell me that you remember smoking on the balcony with him. You elaborate on the memory, telling me the color of our plastic chairs, how we hung our clothes to dry. How there was a small table for an ashtray and sometimes some coffee. Your story is profusely detailed.
I interrupt you, asking if you would also accept my dad’s coat. This catches your attention. You begin to describe his smell. But you stop, and you ask for the third thing.
I think for a long time.
I don’t have a third thing, at least not one that you would want. I offer the headphones, finally, but you respond by asking what you would listen to.
You ask me if I love you. After everything.
I tell you that you I don’t know how to give you that. I set down the cigarettes and the coat, and ask if it is enough.
“Giving me your love is simple,” you say, “you need only come over here, into my arms.”
