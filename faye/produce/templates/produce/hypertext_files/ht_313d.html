“What good is your promise when you ask for none of my stories, or lie to me about your life.” The kabocha’s eyes burn fiercely, but just for a moment.
Time passes.
“I am sorry that I spoke to you in anger,” says the kabocha.
I say nothing back.
“You should leave here soon—it is not a place that people should stay long, especially when they are young and directionless.”
“I cannot offer you anything,” says the kabocha, “but I can wish you a safe journey out of this place. I can watch your passage.”
“Where do I go?” I ask.
“Toward that place, over there,” the kabocha’s flames bend left.
I look, and see a small grove of trees in the distance.
“Where will you go?” I ask.
The kabocha is silent for a while. “I am hollow,” it says, “What does it matter?”
