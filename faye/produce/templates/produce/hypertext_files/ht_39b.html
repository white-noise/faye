We emerge into a clearing. If there was a city around us, I would not have known it. And when I say it was a clearing, I mean there were no large trees. The ground was not flat but rolling, a small hill pushing toward the black sky. Nested in the folds of the hill were gravestones and other stone detritus, maybe the remains of walls, or even older structures on top of which the most recent ruins sat. Long grass shot up around the stones, as if to soften the transition between the hills and the toothy forms.
It was calm, in the way that my thoughts about death often were, at least in contrast to the upheaval I felt was so easily assigned to the topic. Simultaneously the clearing was a process of or movement toward destruction, and a static diorama.
“We are here,” the kabocha says, “Never so long a journey as you think.”
I stand still.
“Be careful not to keep in one place too long,” says the kabocha, “The grass here is strange.”
“Strange?” I say.
“No matter,” the kabocha says, “you must take me over there. Up on the hill. We are almost there.”
I carry it to the hill.
