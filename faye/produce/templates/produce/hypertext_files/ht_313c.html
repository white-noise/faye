“What good is your promise when you lie to me about what you hear.” The kabocha’s eyes burn fiercely, but just for a moment.
Time passes.
“I am sorry that I spoke to you in anger,” says the kabocha, “but your journey is not yet over.”
I say nothing back.
“You should leave here soon—it is not a place that people should stay long, especially when they are young and directionless.”
“Despite your lie,” says the kabocha, “you did ask for my stories. And I can provide you something because of that. It is a piece of advice.”
“What do I do?” I ask.
“You leave the tape recorder with me, here, in this patch of grass.”
“Why would I do that?” I ask.
“Do you think it plays the truth?”
“What does it matter what the truth is, kabocha?”
“Now that is a much better question. And shows how simultaneously important and irrelevant the tape is. How because you are angry to hear me ask for it that it must go.”
I think. And I believe the kabocha. However strange this place is, I believe it.
“Where do I go now?” I ask
“Toward that place, over there,” the kabocha’s flames bend left.
I look, and see a small grove of trees in the distance.
“Where will you go?” I ask.
The kabocha is silent for a while. “I am hollow,” it says, “What does it matter?”
I lay the tape recorder down next to it, taking the headphones out. Its harsh angles are softened by the grass.
I say goodbye.
