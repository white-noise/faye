“You have been relatively honest with me, or at least showed interest in my life, strange as though it is,” says the kabocha.
“I am sorry if I have not been a complete friend to you,” I say.
“You should leave here soon—it is not a place that people should stay long, especially when they are young.”
“What do I do?” I ask.
“Because of your friendliness, I can offer you advice, and an object to help you leave. First, I must ask you to leave the tape recorder with me.”
“Why would I do that?” I ask.
“Do you think it plays the truth?”
“What does it matter what the truth is, kabocha?”
“Now that is a much better question. And shows how simultaneously important and irrelevant the tape is. It is because the question is painful that I ask for it—there are larger pains yet to come. I cannot say more.”
I think. And I believe the kabocha. However strange this place is, I believe it.
I place the recorder, taking the headphones for myself, next to the kabocha, so that its hard angles are softened by the waving grass.
“I can also offer you one of my candles,” the kabocha says.
I do not know what to say. I ask if that will harm it.
“I am made of empty space and light, child,” the kabocha says, “I am barely whole, and am made to he remade. Hollowed out to become whole. Come over and take off my lid—there, pull at the stem.”
I carefully reach inside the kabocha and take its left candle, cuppings its wide base in my hands.
“Now go, over to those trees to the left. Shelter my candle, but do not worry if it must go out. I hope it is not a burden but a comfort to you—remember that my other eye burns here.”
I say goodbye, and begin to walk.
