I grab the biggest coat from the closet in the computer room. It’s my dad’s.
The carpet in here has such long hair. No one has carpets like these anymore. Even so, I’m glad that it hides the sound of my footsteps. Everything in the computer room soaks up sound, which is what makes it seem like the computer is the only thing in there—that, and that I don’t ever turn the lights on. The room’s got one focus.
The stairs are trickier: thin carpet over laminated wood. They’re narrow stairs, and I have to brace myself against the railing to keep my weight off them. In the movies the kid always knows which of the stairs make noise, but here they all make noise. It’s not something someone can come in and just fix.
In a way, I wish my dad would wake up and find me doing this. Chew me out.
But I don’t expect it.
The main floor is blue-black in the darkness. The silhouettes of the living room chairs are like squat little beasts. The kitchen’s stoneware plates, racked up to dry, are like the profiles of faces: noses and brow ridges and earlobes. I slip on my shoes in the entryway.
I open the door, the only thing constantly maintained in this house. It swings outward soundlessly.
