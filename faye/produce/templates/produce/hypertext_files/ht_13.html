I pass under a small highway, vaulted above me on concrete pillars covered in small, square, white tiles. In-between the tiles there is some rust, from the bolts that join the old highway together, built for the olympics that weren’t even held here.
I pass over a bridge and wait for a while as the swollen canal carries bits of wood and leaves toward the bay. If I waited long enough, an entire tree might be washed away like this. In the summer, the water runs clear, and even at night you can almost see the bottom of the canal. You told me this was the only town you had ever lived in with a canal like this. That your hometown canal was dirty, with concrete walls filled with large black stones. You didn’t say it was bad to have a murky canal.
I pass a community center where on Sunday mornings the oldest people I have ever seen sit in their wheelchairs outside the vestibule and talk to one another.
I pass a printing shop with all its lights off but with twenty copiers blinking small green and red lights behind the leaded plate-glass windows.
I pass a closed brick building. It is covered in warning tape. It is overgrown with flowers.
I walk a mile.
I have walked two miles.
