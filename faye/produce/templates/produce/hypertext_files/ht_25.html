“Are those you eyes?” I ask.
“They are how I see, if that is what you are asking.”
“You have flames in your head,” I say, “why are you worried about my flame?”
“I am hoping, of course, that some errant wind will come and blow out my candles. But unfortunately this place is too protected, too quiet, and no storm has ever managed to reach me in this little house. But I have hope, I have time, and I do not want a perfect wind to go to waste just for someone like you to relight my eyes and ruin it all, child.”
I think for a while, unable to find the right question. You never prepared me for this. After a long time, I ask the kabocha if it has to wait long. I say its candles look almost done.
When the kabocha speaks, it does not appear to move, and only the slightest concordance between its eyes’ flicker and voice suggest that it might, somewhere deep in its hollow head, be meaning what it says.
The kabocha speaks animatedly, “Do they really look burned down? That is prime. I will admit that, although I can see many things, I am forbidden from looking at my own eyes. And rarely can I ask about them.”
I assure the kabocha that his eyes are almost gone. Very close. In response his candles flare and he asks, “But, why wait for a wind or for the candles to burn down when you are right here. So rarely does someone find me. Will you blow my eyes out for me? So that I may die?”
