“Just through the passage over there,” says the kabocha. I eye the gap as before, which is bathed in shadow. I have stiffened into my crouch while listening to the kabocha speak, and so when I reach in to take it, cupping my hands around its pebbly sides, I find my knees and shoulders complaining. I am careful not to jostle it. I place it under my arm, draping the coat to protect it; I was not about to be responsible for a draft blowing out the kabocha’s eyes.
“Just through there,” I hear, muffled, from beneath my arm.
“Why do you want to go out there anyway?” I say, “It’s not going to be as peaceful as your little stone home.”
“Because I want to be number one,” says the kabocha, “I want to make a difference.”
Even after a long time it does not elaborate. I take one more look around the shrine. It was not my intention to leave anything, and taking something away feels inappropriate. This circumstance, however, seemed to call for the inappropriate. If you reject the strange, you told me, only more strangeness comes. I have to tent my coat over the kabocha and push away branches hanging over the passage, easing over roots and bits of brick that had come undone from the small path it had pointed out to me.
I don’t see the street coming up ahead.
“Keep going,” the kabocha tells me.
