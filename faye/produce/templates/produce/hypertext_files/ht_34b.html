I play another section from the tape player.
In it, I hear the sound of hissing, metal bumping gently into metal. You telling me to be careful tells me that we are cooking together. I am told to stay away from knives, and to watch you do the cutting. I imagine a huge pile of vegetables and the sounds of cutting, each of them different—long stringy chops, short high squeaks, thunderous snaps, suggest a million kinds of food. I keep asking when you are going to be done, and you tell me to go and play with all the pulp that came out of the pumpkin we are using to make our soup.
When you are done cutting, as I can hear as the woody thumps of the knife fade out, I am instructed to put all of the cubes into the pot of boiling water. I must hold up my pumpkin-goop covered hands because you say, in mock surprise, “Oh no, you’ve killed the kabocha!” You are holding the tape recorder this time, my father nowhere in earshot, and so I hear the hiss of the gas flame and the babbling water as you bring the microphone close to my mouth, and I drop the pieces in, one by one.
You chide me not to eat them but I, having no self control at that age, tell you that the pumpkin is bland and gross. I don’t know how old I am, but my voice is high, and cuts over yours and the complexly interlaced chattering of the kitchen.
“See how it turns bright orange,” you tell me. "How something so scary as hot water can allow such beautiful color to explode?” Then the tape goes silent.
I stop the player.
