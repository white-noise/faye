The kabocha’s eyes flare.
“I am sorry,” says the kabocha.
“Sorry for what?” I ask.
“That you have experienced such loss.”
“What do you know about loss?” I ask the kabocha, “you are just a kabocha.”
It says, “Every day my eyes devour themselves by seeing. My insides harden and crack against the air. I was plucked from a vine. I know many small losses. But none, I admit, so confusing as yours.”
“It’s not confusing,” I say.
“You blame things you don’t even understand,” says the kabocha.
“I blame nothing, kabocha, and I understand enough.”
“Then will you play more of that tape in your pocket?”
“You would listen to things you aren’t meant to hear?” I ask.
The kabocha sits, flickering gently, at moments to me a pumpkin and at others a face, a body, a thing of itself. Its thick green skin puckers around its eyes, curling toward the heat of their twin flames.
The kabocha speaks, “We all hear things we are not meant to. See things we are not meant to. Things we were never even able to see or hear before, such that, in a series of great accidents over the course of our lives, or perhaps by great invention and change (I see few differences), we become accustomed to being misled, coming to find ourselves wanting it. It is a gnawing, you know. It is how you grew up, too. How you learned about kissing. How you learned about death. And now, more than ever, we are subject to secrets from the past, caught on things like your magnetic tape, or in the cramped backsides of cameras. Coiled tightly in dark movie halls. I do not mean to listen to things I am not meant to hear—no I do not—or see things I am not meant to see. But I do, and they make me sad in the way knowing any secret makes one sad. Everyone likes trying to know secrets, not actually knowing them, it seems.”
“Do you know a secret, kabocha?”
“I know many secrets, child. I say that I want to die. How can I not know a secret?”
“People have chosen death without knowing secrets.”
“I know that, child.”
“Then I will carry you where you want to go,” I say.
