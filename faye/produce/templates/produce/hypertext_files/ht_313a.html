“You have been relatively honest with me, or at least shown interest in my life, strange as though it is,” says the kabocha.
“It is all I could do. Other people are the only way I get to see the world outside of this head,” I say. “I will try to be honest.”
“You should leave here soon—it is not a place that people should stay long, especially when they are young and directionless,” the kabocha says.
“What do I do?” I ask.
“Because of your friendliness, I can offer you advice, and some objects to help you leave. First, I must ask you to leave the tape recorder with me.”
“Why would I do that?” I ask. I clutch it in my pocket.
“Do you think it plays the truth?”
“What does it matter what the truth is, kabocha?”
“Now that is a much better question. And shows how simultaneously important and irrelevant the tape is. It is because the question is painful that I ask for it—there are larger pains yet to come. I cannot say more.”
I think. And I believe the kabocha. However strange this place is, I believe it.
I place the recorder, taking the headphones for myself, next to the kabocha, so that its hard angles are softened by the waving grass.
“I can also offer you my candles,” the kabocha says.
I do not know what to say. I ask, awkwardly, if that will harm it.
“I am made of empty space and light, child,” the kabocha says, “I am barely whole, and am made to he remade. Hollowed out to be full. Come over and take off my lid—there, pull at the base.”
I carefully reach inside the kabocha and take its candles, cupping their wide bases in my hands. The wax is cool to the touch, and the flames, close up, seeming to dance faster than everything else around us. I can almost believe that the kabocha can see all it wishes to, though in another sense I can see how this sight could be inflammatory, incessant, and painful.
“Now go, over to those trees to the left. Shelter my candles, but do not worry if they must go out. I hope they are not a burden but a comfort.”
I say goodbye, looking at the pieces of my friend in my hands.
