“Hello, child,” you say to me. Your arms are twisted up in the tree. Your body is supported by its thick limbs. You are part of it, but you are also who I remember.
“Come closer,” you say, “You have come all this way.”
I keep my distance. I ask you what you want. What it will take for me to leave here.
“You would leave your mother?”
You are not my mother. Or, at least, it is more complicated than that. When the world becomes thin, it seems like people–who they are, what they do—can slip from place to place, reflecting off and multiplying in the weak barriers that transect our lives. I can’t tell from where your body reflects now.
“What must I do to leave?”
“You must give me three things.”
I consider what I have. I tell you that I can give you one of the cigarettes in my pocket.
“They are your father’s?” you ask.
I tell you yes, and you accept, and tell me that you remember smoking on the balcony with him. You elaborate on the memory, telling me the color of our plastic chairs, how we hung our clothes to dry. How there was a small table for an ashtray and sometimes some coffee. Your story is profusely detailed.
I interrupt you, asking if you would also accept my dad’s coat. This catches your attention. You begin to describe his smell. But you stop, and you ask for the third thing.
The only thing I have left to offer is the tape recorder. I mention it, and you seem surprised to see that I have it.
“You’ve listened to that and you don’t want to come into my arms? I am here. You have heard all you need to know in my voice.”
I say that I am not sure what I have heard. I am not sure whether it comes from me, you, or something outside of both of us. I ask you whether, if I had agreed, you would have taken me with you.
You are silent for a long time, the tree patiently supporting your body as it had for a long time now. I ask you if that would have been love—to go with you.
You turn your head as far as the thick branches around you with allow. “It is not so simple as that, child. You see this place. You see how it is lonely and dark.”
“I do,” I say.
“Then you can imagine that when the other world looks as bleak as this one—when we hear ourselves recorded, and watch constructed lives on screens, and witness the horrible explosion of images of ourselves and what we should want, what they should be, that it becomes difficult to know what sleep is. What rest is. What living is. Where exactly you are going. Far becomes near and near becomes tangled. And the whole world is ensnared.”
"Will you take the tape, mother?” I ask. I cannot convince you that the world is different than you think.
“Does it have your voice?” you say.
I tell you yes, it does.
You ask me if I love you. After everything.
I tell you that you I am only to give you three things. And that you are both you and not. I set down the cigarettes, and the coat, and the tape player, and ask if it is enough.
You say nothing.
I ask politely to leave.
And like all ghosts, you keep your promises.
