I hear you.
You are telling me about all of the plants in our tiny garden, the one that used to take up half our balcony, sequestered away in a corner. It only got good sun during half the year. I am begging for plants too big for our garden to handle–usually melons or pumpkins. You would relent, though, even I remember that, and the plants would sprout, but they would either die early or crowd out everything else.
“Here, look at this,” you say in the recording, “look at the autumn plants—they’re always my favorite. Do you know why that is?” In the tape I ask why, earnestly.
“It’s because while everything else shies away from the cold, the bleak sky and coughing wind, they are using it to make their fruit sweeter, their branches stronger, themselves more noticeable.”
I ask insistently, in the tape, when they are going to sprout. To me, at that time, all of the plants, without their fruits, would look the same.
“Patience, child,” you say to me, “have I ever done anything to mislead you?”
I turn off the tape recorder. I can almost feel the kabocha regarding me under my arm, its pebbled woody flesh inquiring my thoughts, seemingly by touch alone.
